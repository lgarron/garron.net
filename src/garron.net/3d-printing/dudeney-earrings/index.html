Dudeney Hinged Triangle-Square Earrings


Can you cut a triangle into pieces and turn it into a square?
→
Can you do it with just four pieces?
And can you arrange them so that they stay connected in the process?


Henry Dudeney made this famous as “The Haberdasher’s Puzzle” in the early 1900s, along with a mathematically perfect construction for a solution:


(Image of Dudeney’s construction, from Project Gutenberg.)

Even if you’ve seen the solution before, it can be quite a challenge to construct it!


If you’re on this page, chances are you’ve received some earrings from me. Inspired by a 3D print-in-place model by mathgrrl, when I got my own 3D printer I wanted to see how neat I could possibly make them.

Turns out: fairly neat! I print them as separate pieces with removable tabs to leave behind perfect hinge loops. These allow threading a piece of 1.75mm filament vertically to act as the core of the hinge.

(Rendering of the source geometry in OpenSCAD.)

And I was even able to make some pretty small versions:
(10mm triangle-square models, with a US cent coin for scale.)

But I think the coolest are the earring versions, in the picture at the top of the page.

One big challenge with these models is that the tolerance of the hinge loops in the 3D model is extremely tight due to slicing quirks — about 0.01mm (that’s right, we’re talking 1% of a millimeter). At one point I had trouble printing any good versions, and it took me 60 experiments across half a year to get back to reasonable quality.

At this point they’re pretty robust and make for a great fidget toys and fun earrings. They’re also great for sparking a bit of curiosity about math. If you have some from me, enjoy your hands-on experience of a classic piece of recreational math from over 100 years ago!
